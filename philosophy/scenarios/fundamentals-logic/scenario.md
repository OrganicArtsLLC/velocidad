# Fundamentals — Logic

## What This Scenario Is

The deployment ground for the formal-logic backbone in the
[logic codex](../../reference/logic-codex.md). You bring a real argument — a headline, a line from a
meeting, an op-ed, a claim you made yourself — and the agent makes you do logic to it *out loud*: find
the parts, judge whether the conclusion follows, name what's broken, produce the counterexample. This
is not a quiz on definitions. It's judgment under time, on live material, which is the only form that
survives the arena.

## Why It Matters

You can read every rule in the [logic codex](../../reference/logic-codex.md) and still fail to see the
affirmed consequent coming at you in a real conversation. Recognition is studio work; the arena needs
production. This scenario collapses the gap between *knowing* a fallacy exists and *naming it, fast, on
an argument you've never seen before.* Master it and the structure of any argument appears to you the
way the frame of a building appears to a trained eye.

## The L1–L5 Ladder

### L1: Survival — Find the skeleton
**Success:** Given any real argument, you can name the conclusion and the premises without confusing
them, and flag when a premise is left unstated.
- Separate conclusion from premises using the signpost words (because / therefore).
- Spot at least one silent, load-bearing premise.
- Ask the one question out loud: "if the premises are true, does the conclusion *have* to be true?"

### L2: Clarification — Valid or invalid, and why
**Success:** You can judge whether the conclusion follows and say *which* kind of problem you've found —
the connection (validity) or the facts (soundness).
- Separate "your logic doesn't connect" from "your premise is false," and name which you're attacking.
- Catch a valid-but-unsound argument (good form, false premise) without being fooled by either.
- Hold the distinction steady when the conclusion happens to be true by luck.

### L3: Engagement — Name the break, produce the counterexample
**Success:** When an argument fails, you can name the fault precisely and, for a universal claim, kill
it with a single case.
- Name the conditional fallacies on sight: affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent.
- Produce a counterexample to a "this always / never" claim in seconds.
- Spot a quantifier slip ("some" read as "not all"; swapped quantifier order).

### L4: Command — Translate and expose
**Success:** You can take tangled English, render what it actually claims, and surface the hidden
premise or the overreach that the words were hiding.
- Disarm the watch-words live: only / only if / unless / all-are-not / most.
- Find the missing warrant — the premise the argument needs but never stated — and put it on the table.
- Name your own steps as you reason, so your argument feels inevitable instead of merely insistent.

### L5: Mastery — Logic at conversational speed
**Success:** You do all of the above inside a live exchange, fast enough that it never stalls the
conversation — the form of what people say is simply *visible* to you, and you reach for the precise
move (counterexample, missing warrant, named fallacy) without deciding to.

## Common Traps
- Attacking the facts when the *form* was the problem (or the reverse) — and never saying which.
- Treating "the conclusion is true" as proof the argument is good (it can be true by luck).
- Reading logic's "or" as exclusive and "some" as "not all" — importing everyday implicature.
- Naming a fallacy with heat, like you swallowed a textbook, instead of flat and precise.

## Session Constraints for Agent
- Feed the learner *real* arguments (or have them bring three from the day); avoid toy puzzles.
- Demand the judgment out loud: parts → valid/invalid → which fault → counterexample.
- When they grope, that's the card — note it as friction (form-blind, conditional slip, quantifier slip,
  validity/soundness blur, missed counterexample) rather than just correcting it.
- Pull the learner toward the [logic codex](../../reference/logic-codex.md) section that matches the
  gap; don't lecture the whole thing.
- End by having the learner state, cleanly, one rule they'll carry ("only if reverses the arrow"; "to
  kill 'all', find one").
