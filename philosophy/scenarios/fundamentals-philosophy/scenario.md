# Fundamentals — Philosophy

## What This Scenario Is

The deployment ground for the canon, loaded as ammunition (see
[positions-of-the-greats](../../reference/positions-of-the-greats.md) and the thinker files beside it).
You take a thinker from the canon and the agent makes you *produce* their position — not recite a
slogan, but state the claim, supply the warrant (why they held it), and name the strongest objection —
then defend or refine it under questioning. The canon stops being names to drop and becomes ammunition
you can fire in one line.

## Why It Matters

A position you've only read is recognition; it jams the moment a live mind pushes back. A position you
*own* — claim, warrant, and objection, cold, in under ten seconds — is a pre-built argument you can
deploy. This scenario feeds [Domain 5 of the discourse codex](../../reference/discourse-codex.md)
directly: the arsenal you carry into the arena.

## The L1–L5 Ladder

### L1: Survival — State the position
**Success:** You can state a named thinker's actual position in 2–3 clean sentences, without notes and
without blurring it into a popular caricature.
- Get the position right (Stoicism ≠ "stay calm"; Descartes' doubt is methodic, not idle skepticism).
- State a claim definitely, before hedging.
- Notice when you're reaching for the slogan instead of the actual view.

### L2: Clarification — Supply the warrant
**Success:** You can say *why* the thinker held the view — the reasoning, not just the conclusion — and
give one piece of support.
- Attach the warrant in the same breath as the claim ("…because the sensible world won't sit still").
- Distinguish the thinker's real argument from a popularized version of it.
- Locate the problem the position was built to solve.

### L3: Engagement — Name the objection, answer it
**Success:** You can state the strongest standing objection to the view and give a first-pass answer.
- Steelman the objection before answering it (no strawmen).
- Give the thinker's own response where they have one (Plato on the Third Man; Kant on the thing-in-itself).
- Concede honestly when the objection lands, and say how it refines the position.

### L4: Command — Across thinkers and eras
**Success:** You can hold a multi-turn exchange that moves between thinkers, tracing how one answers,
extends, or demolishes another.
- Connect positions across the canon (Aristotle answering Plato; Nietzsche answering Schopenhauer).
- Deploy a position as a live move (the Stoic dichotomy to re-sort a grievance; the golden mean to
  dissolve a false binary).
- Defend a view you don't personally hold, faithfully, then state where you actually land.

### L5: Mastery — The canon as ammunition
**Success:** You own the core positions cold — claim, warrant, objection, under ten seconds each — and
reach for the right one inside a real disagreement, including changing your mind when the argument
demands it.

## Common Traps
- Giving the slogan instead of the argument ("the unexamined life…" with no account of *why*).
- Misattribution — assigning a position to the wrong thinker, or blurring the real view with the popular one.
- Strawmanning the objection so it's easy to answer.
- Performing erudition — name-dropping to sound learned instead of deploying the structure to do work.

## Session Constraints for Agent
- Play a sharp, fair interlocutor: demand the warrant, supply counterexamples, refuse assertion without support.
- Hold the canon honest — flag misattributions and popularized blurs immediately (see study-config).
- Force at least two concessions or refinements; intellectual honesty is the trained move.
- Pull the learner to the matching [reference file](../../reference/) when a gap shows.
- End with the learner stating the position — and its strongest objection — cleanly, in under ten seconds.
