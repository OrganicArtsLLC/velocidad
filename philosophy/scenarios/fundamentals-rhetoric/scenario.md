# Fundamentals — Rhetoric

## What This Scenario Is

The deployment ground for the [discourse codex](../../reference/discourse-codex.md). The agent plays an
interlocutor who pushes — not to win, but to apply pressure — while you practice the basic live moves:
take the pause, receive one thread without drowning, locate the real disagreement, and make one clean
move instead of blurting five. This is the arena at training weight.

## Why It Matters

The [discourse codex](../../reference/discourse-codex.md) is the map; this is the first time you walk
the ground. Composure, reception, and orientation are reflexes, and reflexes are built only under live
pressure — you cannot read your way to them. This scenario is where the flood stops being a flood and
starts being *structure arriving, one thread at a time, that you have the moves to meet.*

## The L1–L5 Ladder

### L1: Survival — Stand and receive
**Success:** Under a push, you can take a deliberate pause and restate the other side's single
load-bearing claim before you respond at all.
- Take a two-second chosen pause without it leaking as hesitation (um, eyes up, rising voice).
- Restate one claim the other person made, accurately, before answering.
- Sort fast into not-yours (their tone, their move) vs. yours (your judgment, your next sentence).

### L2: Clarification — Steelman and pin
**Success:** You can state the other view at its strongest, and stop an argument over a loaded word by
pinning its meaning.
- Steelman: "the strongest version of what you're saying is ___" — and have them endorse it.
- Pin a term: "what do you mean by ___?" before arguing a loaded word (fair, justice, success).
- Separate the argument from the heat: answer the claim, acknowledge the feeling without conceding.

### L3: Engagement — Locate the real disagreement
**Success:** You can name *where* the disagreement actually lives before spending words on it.
- Relocate the stasis: "are we disagreeing about whether it happened, or whether it was justified?"
- Find the missing warrant: "that follows only if we assume ___ — do we grant that?"
- Hold one thread to completion instead of trying to answer everything at once.

### L4: Command — The moves, to reflex
**Success:** You can reach for the right one of the seven core moves under pressure, and concede a real
point precisely instead of digging in.
- Deploy restate-then-go, name-the-warrant, relocate-stasis, pin-the-term, steelman-then-rebut,
  concede-precisely, ask-for-the-counterexample — each as a single sentence.
- Make a precise concession that sharpens your claim ("you're right that ___; that refines my view to ___").
- Wrap your logos: lead with ethos (listen, concede), then the structured point, then why it matters to them.

### L5: Mastery — Steer the live current
**Success:** In a real disagreement, you stay composed, receive without drowning, find the real fight,
and move the other mind — and you change your own when the argument demands it. Another mind coming at
you fast registers as structure, not turbulence.

## Common Traps
- Answering the instant they stop talking (the arena's worst lie).
- Pre-composing your rebuttal while they're still talking — so you answer what you assumed, not what they said.
- Trying to refute everything; the load-bearing claim is usually one thing and the rest is scaffolding.
- All-logos delivery: airtight reasoning, zero ethos or pathos, persuading no one.

## Session Constraints for Agent
- Play a respectful, relentless, precise interlocutor — pressure, not hostility. One push at a time, then wait.
- Reward the pause and the restate; flag every blurt, every pre-composed answer, every missed thread as friction.
- Force at least two moments where the learner must concede or refine rather than defend out of momentum.
- Point to the matching [codex](../../reference/discourse-codex.md) domain when a gap shows; don't lecture.
- End with the learner stating their refined position in 2–3 clean sentences, and one move they'll drill to reflex.
