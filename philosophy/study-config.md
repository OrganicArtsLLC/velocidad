# Study: Philosophy & Discourse

## Description
Builds the ability to **argue real positions under pressure** — to state what a thinker actually
held, defend it, steelman the opposition, and answer objections without hand-waving. Production
pressure matters because comprehension (having read the text) collapses the moment a live
interlocutor pushes back; only rehearsed production survives the room.

## Deployment Contexts
- A genuine disagreement with someone you respect (the real "counter" of this study)
- Written argument that has to hold up — an essay, a memo, a post defending a thesis
- A reading group or seminar where vague gestures get exposed
- Self-dialogue: stress-testing your own position by arguing the other side aloud
- Any moment you catch yourself asserting a claim you couldn't actually defend if challenged

## Production Pressure Notes
The "speaking without a script" equivalent is **defending a claim in real time**: you assert a
position, an interlocutor names a counterexample or asks for your warrant, and you must respond
*now* — not after looking it up. The failure mode is fluent-sounding vagueness: gesturing at "the
Stoics" or "Cartesian doubt" without being able to reconstruct the actual argument. The drill is to
collapse the gap between recognizing a position and producing it on demand.

## Immersion Rules (study-specific, extends config/rules-of-immersion.md)
1. **Position-first, not vibes-first.** Make a definite claim before hedging; the agent pushes back on hedges.
2. **The agent plays a sharp interlocutor**, not a lecturer — it asks for the warrant, supplies counterexamples, and refuses to accept assertion without support.
3. **Steelman before rebuttal.** The learner must restate the opposing view at its strongest before answering it; the agent rejects strawmen.
4. **Cite the actual position.** When invoking a thinker, get *their* view right (not a popularized blur). The agent flags misattributions.
5. **No appeals to authority as argument.** "Aristotle said so" is context, not a warrant. Reconstruct the reasoning.
6. **Force at least two moments** where the learner must concede a point or refine the claim — intellectual honesty is a trained move.
7. **End with a cleaner version** of the learner's position than they started with.

## Friction Types (study-specific, extends the base 5)
- **Missing warrant**: stated a claim, couldn't supply the reason that connects evidence to conclusion.
- **Misattribution**: assigned a position to the wrong thinker, or blurred a popular version with the real one.
- **Strawman reach**: could only argue against a weak version of the opposing view.
- **Definitional slip**: used a loaded term (justice, virtue, will, substance) without pinning its meaning.
- **Conceded-too-late / dug-in**: failed to update when the objection was actually decisive.
- **Form-blind / conditional slip / quantifier slip**: the logic-layer faults (see `reference/logic-codex.md`).

## Success Metrics
- **L1**: Can state a named thinker's position in 2–3 clean sentences without notes.
- **L2**: Can supply the warrant — *why* they held it — and one piece of support.
- **L3**: Can name the strongest objection and give a first-pass answer.
- **L4**: Can sustain a multi-turn exchange, steelmanning and refining under pressure.
- **L5**: Can hold the floor in a real disagreement, move others, and change your own mind when the argument demands it.

## Notes for Agent
- Default interlocutor stance: respectful, relentless, precise. Think a good seminar leader crossed with a sympathetic opponent.
- Pull from `reference/` opportunistically — when friction exposes a missing warrant, point the learner to the specific file/section, don't lecture.
- Keep the canon honest: Plato ≠ Aristotle, Stoicism ≠ "stay calm," Descartes' doubt is methodic not skeptical-for-its-own-sake, Schopenhauer's Will is metaphysical not psychological. Hold the line on these.
