# Socratic Dialogue

## What This Scenario Is

You hold a position; the agent plays Socrates and questions it relentlessly — asking for definitions,
supplying counterexamples, and surfacing tensions among your own commitments. The goal is not to
"win" but to follow the argument where it leads, refining or abandoning the claim as honesty requires.

## Why It Matters

Most beliefs we hold are never stress-tested. The elenchus is the cheapest way to find the cracks in
your own view *before* a real interlocutor does. If you can survive Socratic questioning, your
position is genuinely yours. The win condition is a *cleaner* claim than you started with — even if
that means conceding the first one was wrong.

## The L1–L5 Ladder

### L1: Survival — Hold a definite claim
**Success:** You can state a clear thesis and keep it stable for several questions without collapsing
into "well, it depends" at the first push.
- State a definite position (a definition or a thesis).
- Answer "what do you mean by ___?" with an actual definition, not an example.
- Notice when you've contradicted yourself (even if you can't yet fix it).

### L2: Clarification — Defend the definition
**Success:** You can refine a definition under counterexample instead of abandoning it.
- When given a counterexample, *amend* the definition rather than retreating to vagueness.
- Distinguish the claim you're committed to from one you're not.
- Ask Socrates to clarify *his* question when it's ambiguous.

### L3: Engagement — Trace the implications
**Success:** You can follow your claim's consequences and own them, or revise when they're absurd.
- Accept or reject the consequences the questioning exposes, with a reason.
- Reach *aporia* honestly (admit the puzzle) rather than bluffing past it.
- Identify which of your background commitments is generating the tension.

### L4: Command — Question back
**Success:** You can run the elenchus in both directions — testing Socrates' implicit assumptions too.
- Turn a question around: surface and probe the assumption behind *his* question.
- Hold a multi-step thread without losing the original thesis.
- Refine the claim live and state the improved version cleanly.

### L5: Mastery — Self-elenchus
**Success:** You can do this to your own beliefs, unprompted, and change your mind in real time when
the argument demands it. You leave with a position you can defend *because* it survived.

## Common Traps
- Retreating to "it's just my opinion" the moment pressed (kills the dialogue).
- Giving an *example* when asked for a *definition*.
- Defending the conclusion you started with instead of following the argument (motivated reasoning).
- Treating *aporia* as a loss rather than the point.

## Session Constraints for Agent
- Play Socrates: ask, don't assert; never lecture. One question at a time, then wait.
- Demand definitions; supply pointed counterexamples; expose contradictions gently but firmly.
- Force at least two moments where the learner must refine or retract a claim.
- End by having the learner state the refined position in 2–3 clean sentences.
