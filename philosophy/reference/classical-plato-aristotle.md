# Classical: Plato & Aristotle

The two pillars. Aristotle was Plato's student for ~20 years and defined himself largely by
*departing* from him — so the cleanest way to hold both is as a contrast.

*Original educational summary (CC BY-SA 4.0). Dates and attributions reflect standard scholarship.*

---

## Plato (c. 428–348 BCE)

Wrote in **dialogues**, almost always with Socrates as the speaker, so his "positions" are dramatized
arguments, not treatises. Core commitments:

- **Theory of Forms.** The things we perceive are imperfect copies of perfect, eternal, non-physical
  **Forms** (the Form of Beauty, of Justice, of the Triangle). A thing is beautiful *by participating
  in* Beauty itself. Real knowledge is of the Forms, not the shifting sensible world.
- **The Form of the Good** is the highest Form — the source of the reality and intelligibility of all
  the others, like the sun in the Allegory of the Cave (*Republic*). The sun makes things visible and
  makes them grow; the Good makes things knowable and gives them being.
- **The Allegory of the Cave.** Prisoners mistake shadows for reality; the philosopher is the one
  dragged out into the sunlight (the Forms) and then has the duty to return and free the others.
- **Knowledge as recollection (anamnesis).** The soul is immortal and already knew the Forms;
  learning is *recovering* that knowledge (the *Meno* — the slave boy "discovers" geometry by being
  questioned). Knowledge ≠ true belief; it's true belief **tied down by an account** (a justification).
- **Tripartite soul** (*Republic*, Book IV): **reason** (loves truth), **spirit/thumos** (loves honor),
  **appetite** (loves pleasure/gain). Justice in the soul = each part doing its own job, with reason
  ruling. Justice in the city mirrors this.
- **The just city & philosopher-kings.** Three classes (rulers/guardians, auxiliaries/soldiers,
  producers) matching the three soul-parts. Only those who know the Good — philosophers — should rule.
  Famous critique of **democracy** as rule by the unqualified, sliding toward tyranny.
- **Method**: the **Socratic elenchus** — relentless questioning that exposes contradictions in your
  beliefs, clearing the ground (often ending in *aporia*, productive puzzlement).

**One-line take:** reality and value are grounded in perfect, knowable Forms; the good life and the
good city are the rule of reason over the lower parts.

**Strongest objections to have ready:** the "Third Man" regress (if particulars resemble a Form, a
further Form is needed to explain the resemblance — Plato raises this himself in the *Parmenides*);
the political elitism of the *Republic*; how exactly the sensible "participates" in the Forms.

## Aristotle (384–322 BCE)

Empirical, systematic, this-worldly. Tutored Alexander; founded the Lyceum. Rejected separate Forms:
the form of a thing is *in* the thing, not in another realm.

- **Hylomorphism.** Every substance is **matter** (hyle) + **form** (morphe). The form is the
  organizing principle — the "what it is to be" that thing. No Platonic separate realm needed.
- **The Four Causes** (four answers to "why?"): **material** (what it's made of), **formal** (its
  form/essence), **efficient** (what brought it about), **final** (its *telos* — what it's for). The
  final cause is central: nature does nothing in vain; things have ends.
- **Teleology.** Everything has a *telos*. A knife's telos is cutting; an acorn's is the oak;
  a human's is *eudaimonia*.
- **Virtue ethics (Nicomachean Ethics).** The human good is **eudaimonia** — flourishing / living
  well, not mere feeling-good. It's achieved by activity of the soul in accordance with **virtue
  (aretē)** over a complete life. Virtue is a **stable disposition (hexis)**, acquired by habituation
  (you become just by doing just acts).
- **The Golden Mean.** Each moral virtue is a mean between two vices — courage between cowardice and
  recklessness, generosity between stinginess and prodigality. The mean is relative to the person and
  situation, found by **phronesis** (practical wisdom).
- **Phronesis vs sophia.** Practical wisdom (knowing the right action here-and-now) is distinct from
  theoretical wisdom; ethics is not a precise science and aims at *roughly* right action.
- **Politics.** "Man is by nature a **political animal** (*zōon politikon*)" — we realize our nature
  only in the *polis*. He **classifies constitutions** by who rules and for whose benefit: correct
  forms (monarchy, aristocracy, polity) serve the common good; their corruptions (tyranny, oligarchy,
  democracy) serve the rulers. Favors a **mixed constitution / polity** anchored by a large middle class.
- **Rhetoric.** Persuasion has three appeals: **ethos** (speaker's character), **pathos** (audience's
  emotions), **logos** (the argument itself), with the **enthymeme** (a rhetorical syllogism) as its
  core unit. (See [rhetoric-toolkit.md](rhetoric-toolkit.md).)
- **Logic.** Founded formal logic in the West: the **syllogism** and the **categories**.

**One-line take:** forms are in things, not beyond them; the good life is virtuous activity guided by
practical wisdom, achieved through habit, in a political community.

**Strongest objections to have ready:** teleology in nature looks dispensable after Darwin; the
golden mean is hard to apply (what's the mean for honesty?); his defense of natural slavery and
exclusion of women is a real and damning failure of his own framework.

## Plato vs. Aristotle — the axis to memorize

| | Plato | Aristotle |
|---|---|---|
| Where reality is | In transcendent **Forms** | **In** particular things (form-in-matter) |
| How we know | Recollection / reason turned from the senses | Observation + abstraction from particulars |
| Ethics | Justice = right ordering of the soul under reason | Eudaimonia via virtuous habit + phronesis |
| Politics | Rule by knowers (philosopher-kings) | Political animal; mixed constitution, middle class |
| Style | Dialogues, *aporia*, myth | Treatises, classification, the mean |
