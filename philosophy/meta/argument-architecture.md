# Argument Architecture

Reverse-engineering discourse as a system. Not a list of rules — the operating model under every move.

This is the meta-system: how argument *actually works* when a mind is coming at you in real time,
decomposed into layers you can drill one at a time. Once you see the machine, you stop improvising blind
and start reaching for the right part.

**Design philosophy:** an argument is a runtime. The arena is the live execution environment. You are
learning its instruction set (the moves), its memory model (one thread at a time), and its error
handling (composure and repair). The studio is where you read the manual; the arena is where the program
runs.

---

## The Machine Model

A live exchange is two minds passing force through a lossy medium (language). Five subsystems run at
once. Map them and every failure becomes locatable instead of a vague "I froze."

| Layer | What it is | The codex domain | Fails as |
|-------|-----------|------------------|----------|
| **1. Composure** | Staying online under pressure | Domain 1 | flooding, blurting, leaking hesitation |
| **2. Reception** | Taking the other mind in accurately | Domain 2 | drowning, answering what you assumed |
| **3. Orientation** | Locating the real disagreement | Domain 3 | arguing past each other at different stases |
| **4. Production** | The moves you actually say | Domain 4 | all-logos, dug-in, re-asserting louder |
| **5. Arsenal** | The positions/fallacies you carry loaded | Domain 5 | recognition that jams; performing erudition |

The full live treatment is in [`../reference/discourse-codex.md`](../reference/discourse-codex.md). The
formal floor under Layers 3–5 is in [`../reference/logic-codex.md`](../reference/logic-codex.md).

## Layer 1 — Composure (the power supply)

Nothing else runs if this is down. The two load-bearing parts:

- **The dichotomy of control.** Sort every incoming signal into *yours* (your judgment, your next
  sentence, your pace) and *not yours* (their tone, their move, whether they concede). Effort goes only
  to the first bin. This is the same Stoic move from `stoics-seneca-marcus.md`, pointed at a conversation.
- **The chosen pause.** Two seconds of silence reads as gravity. The pause is where reception finishes
  and production gets *chosen* instead of blurted. A leaked pause (um, eyes up) reads as weakness; a
  chosen one reads as command. Train the chosen one until it overwrites the leak.

---

## Layer 2 — Reception (the input buffer)

The arena floods here first, because another mind's thought arrives faster than you can file it. The fix
is structural, not a matter of being smarter:

- **One-thread rule.** Working memory holds about three items; an argument throws six at you. Hold
  exactly *one* thread — theirs — to completion, then answer one piece of it. You almost never need to
  answer everything.
- **Compress to claim + warrant.** A three-minute point is usually one claim, one reason, and a lot of
  field-noise. Store the *structure*, not the words.
- **Steelman as reception.** Restating their view at its strongest is the best listening instrument you
  have — you can't strengthen what you only half-heard.

---

## Layer 3 — Orientation (the router)

Before arguing, find out what you're arguing about. Three cheap moves that save expensive ones:

- **Stasis** — is the disagreement about *fact*, *definition*, *quality*, or *policy*? Fighting at
  different stases is the most common source of heat-with-no-motion.
- **Pin the term** — a loaded word meaning two things is field-noise mistaken for disagreement. Half of
  fights dissolve when the term is pinned.
- **Find the missing warrant** — most real arguments are enthymemes (a hidden premise). Naming it is the
  sharpest structural move because it doesn't attack the person; it asks the argument to show its parts.

---

## Layer 4 — Production (the instruction set)

Seven moves, each a single sentence, each pre-built and cold (see the chunk reference). The arena gives
you the three seconds it takes to *say* a move, not the thirty it takes to *find* one — so they must be
reflex. The two counter-intuitive ones:

- **Concession is a weapon.** Granting the true part while sharpening your claim buys ethos, disarms,
  and narrows the fight to ground you can hold. The failure mode is conceding too late out of momentum.
- **Wrap the logos.** Lead with ethos (listen, concede), then the structured point, then pathos (why it
  matters to *them*). Pure logos delivered flat persuades no one.

---

## Layer 5 — Arsenal (the loaded library)

Positions and fallacies, drilled cold, become one-line moves. A position is *owned* only when you can
state it with its **warrant and its strongest objection in under ten seconds** — conclusion-only is
recognition, and recognition jams under pressure. The library lives in `../reference/`; the SRS deck in
`../srs/` is how it goes cold.

---

## The Unlock Sequence

Build the layers bottom-up; each rests on the one below.

1. **Composure first.** Drill the pause and the dichotomy of control until pressure stops flooding you.
2. **Then reception.** The one-thread rule and the steelman, until another mind's sprawl arrives as
   structure, one thread at a time.
3. **Then orientation.** Stasis, pin-the-term, missing-warrant — until you locate the real fight before
   spending a word.
4. **Then production.** The seven moves to reflex; concession as a weapon; wrapping the logos.
5. **Then arsenal.** The canon and the fallacies, cold, deployed as moves rather than displayed as
   knowledge.

You'll know the machine is built when a fast, sharp interlocutor registers not as turbulence but as
*structure arriving* — and your hand is already on the right part.
