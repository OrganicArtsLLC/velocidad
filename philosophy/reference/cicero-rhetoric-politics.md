# Cicero: Rhetoric, Natural Law & the Republic

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) — Roman statesman, lawyer, and the bridge that carried Greek
philosophy into Latin (and thus into the West). Not a system-builder; an **eclectic** who took the
best from each school (skeptical Academy in epistemology, Stoicism in ethics/politics). His genius is
**rhetoric** and **political philosophy**, and the union of the two: the ideal is the
*philosopher-statesman* who can both reason and persuade.

## Rhetoric — the Five Canons

Cicero (with the *Rhetorica ad Herennium* tradition and *De Oratore*) transmitted the **five canons of
rhetoric**, the production pipeline for any persuasive case. Memorize these — they're the backbone of
the `rhetorical-composition` scenario:

1. **Inventio (invention)** — discovering what to say: the arguments, evidence, and appeals available
   for this case. (Includes finding ethos/pathos/logos and the right *stasis* — see [rhetoric-toolkit.md](rhetoric-toolkit.md).)
2. **Dispositio (arrangement)** — ordering it: classically *exordium* (intro/hook + ethos) →
   *narratio* (the facts) → *partitio* (roadmap) → *confirmatio* (your proofs) → *refutatio* (rebut
   theirs) → *peroratio* (conclusion + pathos).
3. **Elocutio (style)** — putting it into words: clarity, correctness, ornament, appropriateness;
   matching the **three styles** (plain to teach, middle to please, grand to move).
4. **Memoria (memory)** — holding it for delivery without reading it; the method of loci lives here.
5. **Pronuntiatio / actio (delivery)** — voice, pacing, gesture. Cicero (echoing Demosthenes) rated
   delivery decisive.

**The orator's three duties:** *docere* (to teach/prove — logos), *delectare* (to please — ethos),
*movere* (to move — pathos).

Cicero's ideal orator (*De Oratore*) is **not a technician** but a broadly educated person — you
can't persuade well about justice, war, or law without actually understanding them. Rhetoric without
wisdom is dangerous; wisdom without eloquence is politically inert.

## Natural Law

In *De Re Publica* and *De Legibus*, Cicero gives the West its classic statement of **natural law**:

> "True law is right reason in agreement with nature; it is of universal application, unchanging and
> everlasting... It is not allowed to alter this law, nor to repeal any part of it..." (*De Re Publica* III)

- Law is grounded in **reason and nature**, not mere convention or the will of the powerful.
- It is **universal and unchangeable** — the same in Rome and Athens, now and always.
- An unjust "law" that violates it is not truly law. (This line runs toward Aquinas, the natural-rights
  tradition, and modern human-rights thinking.)
- Justice is therefore **not** merely "the interest of the stronger" (the position Plato's
  *Republic* attacks via Thrasymachus) — it has a real, rational foundation.

## The Republic & the Mixed Constitution

- **Res publica** = "the property/affair of the people" (*res populi*) — a people united by shared
  justice and common good. The state belongs to the citizens, not a ruler.
- **Mixed constitution.** Following Polybius, Cicero argues the most stable government **blends**
  monarchy (consuls), aristocracy (senate), and democracy (assemblies) so each checks the others —
  he saw the Roman Republic at its best as this balance. (An ancestor of separation-of-powers thinking.)
- **De Officiis (On Duties):** practical Stoic-flavored ethics for public life — the honorable
  (*honestum*) and the useful (*utile*) never truly conflict; apparent conflicts dissolve under
  scrutiny. The statesman owes duties to country, family, and humanity.
- Cicero **died defending the Republic** — proscribed and killed in 43 BCE after attacking Mark Antony
  in the *Philippics*. The biography is part of the position: he staked his life on the claim that a
  republic of laws beats the rule of a strongman.

## One-line takes

- **Rhetoric:** persuasion is a craft with five stages (invent, arrange, style, memorize, deliver) and
  three duties (teach, please, move) — but only legitimate when joined to wisdom.
- **Natural law:** real justice is right reason in accord with nature, universal and above any ruler's decree.
- **Politics:** the republic belongs to the people; stability comes from a mixed, balanced constitution.

**Strongest objections to have ready:** natural law assumes a rational, normative cosmos many moderns
reject; the "mixed constitution" idealizes a Roman system that still excluded most people; rhetoric's
power to persuade is exactly what makes it dangerous when *not* yoked to truth (Plato's worry in the
*Gorgias*).
