# Moderns: Descartes & Schopenhauer

Two hinge figures. Descartes opens modern philosophy by relocating certainty from the world to the
**thinking subject**. Schopenhauer, two centuries later and downstream of Kant, locates the inner
nature of everything in a blind **Will** — and turns the optimism of the Enlightenment on its head.

## René Descartes (1596–1650) — the father of modern philosophy

A mathematician (Cartesian coordinates) seeking the same certainty for philosophy that geometry has.
Chief text: *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641).

- **Method of doubt (methodic, not idle skepticism).** To find an unshakable foundation, deliberately
  doubt *everything* that can possibly be doubted, and keep only what survives. The senses deceive;
  he might be dreaming; an **evil demon** might be deceiving him about even math.
- **The cogito.** One thing survives the demon: while I am being deceived, *I am thinking*, so I must
  exist. **"Cogito, ergo sum"** — I think, therefore I am. The thinking self is the first certainty,
  known directly, prior to any knowledge of the body or world.
- **Clear and distinct ideas** are the mark of truth: whatever I perceive as clearly and distinctly as
  the cogito, I can accept.
- **God as guarantor.** From the idea of a perfect being he argues God exists and is no deceiver;
  therefore my clear-and-distinct faculties, used carefully, are reliable — which re-secures the
  external world and science. (Critics since have called this the **"Cartesian circle"**: he seems to
  use clear-and-distinct ideas to prove God, and God to validate clear-and-distinct ideas.)
- **Mind–body dualism (substance dualism).** Two distinct substances: **mind** (res cogitans —
  thinking, unextended) and **body/matter** (res extensa — extended, mechanical). The mind is not the
  brain. This bequeaths the **mind–body problem**: how do an immaterial mind and a physical body
  interact? (Descartes' gesture at the pineal gland satisfied almost no one.)
- **Foundationalism + rationalism.** Knowledge is a structure built on indubitable foundations by
  reason; the senses are secondary. He's a headwater of the **rationalist** stream (vs. the British
  empiricists).

**One-line take:** doubt everything until you hit what can't be doubted — the thinking self — and
rebuild knowledge on that foundation by reason; mind and matter are distinct substances.

**Strongest objections to have ready:** the Cartesian circle; the interaction problem for dualism;
the cogito may only license "thinking is occurring," not a persisting "I" (Hume, Lichtenberg);
foundationalism itself is contested.

## Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) — the philosopher of the Will

Wrote *The World as Will and Representation* (1818). Took **Kant's** distinction between the world as
it appears (phenomenon) and as it is in itself (the thing-in-itself, *Ding an sich*) and made a bold
claim Kant refused: **we can know the thing-in-itself, and it is Will.**

- **The world as representation.** Everything I experience is *my representation* — structured by my
  mind in space, time, and causality (the **principle of sufficient reason**). This is the world as
  phenomenon, broadly Kantian.
- **The world as Will.** But I know myself from the *inside* too — and there I find not an idea but a
  striving: **willing**. Generalizing, the inner nature of *everything* — gravity, growth, instinct,
  desire — is one blind, purposeless, insatiable **Will**. Not a rational God's will; a striving with
  no goal beyond more striving.
- **Pessimism.** Because the Will is endless craving, life is structurally **suffering**: we want;
  satisfaction is brief and breeds new wants or boredom; the cycle never resolves. "Life swings like a
  pendulum between pain and boredom." This is a *consequence* of the metaphysics, not a mood.
- **Two escapes / palliatives:**
  1. **Aesthetic contemplation.** In art (above all **music**, which he held to be a direct copy of
     the Will itself), we briefly become a "pure, will-less subject of knowing" — desire quiets, and
     suffering with it.
  2. **Compassion (Mitleid) and asceticism.** Lasting release comes from seeing through the
     individuation that divides us — recognizing the one Will in all beings — which grounds **morality
     in compassion** and, at the limit, in the **denial of the will-to-live** (asceticism, ego-loss),
     a move he explicitly links to Indian (Vedanta/Buddhist) thought.
- **Ethics grounded in compassion, not reason or duty.** Against Kant's duty-based ethics: the basis
  of morality is *Mitleid* — feeling another's suffering as one's own because, metaphysically, we are
  one Will. The cardinal virtues are justice and loving-kindness; the root vice is egoism.

**One-line take:** behind the world we perceive is a single blind, striving Will; because willing is
endless craving, existence is suffering — and the only relief is to quiet the will, through art and
through compassion that sees past the illusion of separateness.

**Strongest objections to have ready:** the inference from "my inner nature is will" to "*everything's*
inner nature is Will" is a huge leap; if all is one Will, what individuates sufferers?; the pessimism
is contestable (Nietzsche, his heir, inverts it into life-affirmation); calling music a "copy of the
Will" is evocative but hard to cash out.

## The axis: certainty inward (Descartes) vs. reality as Will (Schopenhauer)

| | Descartes | Schopenhauer |
|---|---|---|
| Starting move | Doubt → the thinking self | Kant's phenomenon / thing-in-itself |
| Inner nature of reality | Mind (res cogitans) + matter (res extensa) | One blind **Will** |
| Mood | Confident, foundational, optimistic about reason | Pessimistic; reason is the Will's servant |
| The good | Knowledge rebuilt on certainty | Quieting the will: art, compassion, asceticism |
| Ethics | (Largely outside the *Meditations*) | Compassion (Mitleid), not duty or self-interest |
