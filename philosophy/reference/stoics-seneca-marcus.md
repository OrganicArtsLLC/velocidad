# The Stoics: System, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius

Stoicism is a complete philosophy (logic + physics + ethics), but the part that survives in practice
is the **ethics**: how to live well given what you can and can't control. Founded by Zeno of Citium
(c. 300 BCE); the Roman phase (Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus) is the practical one.

## The Stoic System (the frame everything hangs on)

- **The dichotomy of control** (Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1 — the load-bearing idea): some things are
  **up to us** (our judgments, desires, choices — *prohairesis*) and some are **not** (body, wealth,
  reputation, outcomes, other people). Anxiety comes from trying to control the latter. Direct effort
  only at the former.
- **Virtue is the only good.** The four cardinal virtues — **wisdom, justice, courage, temperance** —
  are the sole good; vice the sole evil. Everything else (health, wealth, even life) is a
  **"preferred" or "dispreferred" indifferent**: worth pursuing or avoiding, but not what makes a life
  good or bad.
- **Live in accordance with Nature / Logos.** The cosmos is rational and providentially ordered
  (*Logos*). Living well = aligning your will with how things actually are, not how you wish they were.
- **Emotions are judgments.** Destructive passions (anger, fear, craving) arise from *false value
  judgments* — treating indifferents as goods/evils. Correct the judgment, and the passion dissolves.
  The goal is **apatheia** (freedom from destructive passion), *not* coldness — Stoics affirm "good
  feelings" (eupatheiai) like rational joy.
- **Amor fati & the discipline of assent.** Accept what happens as part of the whole; control the one
  thing that's yours — assent to impressions. "It's not things that disturb us, but our judgments
  about things" (Epictetus).
- **Practices:** *premeditatio malorum* (rehearse misfortune in advance), the **view from above**
  (zoom out to cosmic scale), *memento mori* (keep death in view to value the present), the evening
  review (audit the day).

## Seneca (c. 4 BCE – 65 CE)

Roman statesman, playwright, Nero's tutor, fabulously wealthy — and aware of the tension between his
life and his creed. Best read in the **Letters to Lucilius** and the short essays.

- **On the Shortness of Life (*De Brevitate Vitae*):** life isn't short — we waste it. "It is not
  that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a lot of it." Most people are busy with
  everything except living.
- **On Anger (*De Ira*):** anger is temporary madness and never useful; better to prevent it than
  manage it. A model Stoic treatment of an emotion as a correctable judgment.
- **Wealth as a preferred indifferent:** he defends possessing wealth while not being *possessed* by
  it — the sage can be rich, but holds it loosely ("the wise man does not love wealth, but prefers it").
- **Memento mori & rehearsing adversity:** "Let us prepare our minds as if we'd come to the very end
  of life... He who has learned to die has unlearned to be a slave."
- **Practical, warm, aphoristic** — Stoicism as letters from a friend, not a treatise.

**One-line take:** time is the only true scarcity; spend it on living virtuously, hold externals
loosely, and rehearse loss so it can't unmake you.

## Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE)

Roman emperor; the *Meditations* are his **private journal** — Stoic exercises written to himself,
never meant for publication. That's why it reads as repetition: it's practice, not doctrine.

- **The dichotomy of control, applied to power.** The most powerful man in the world repeatedly
  reminds himself what is *not* in his control and that his only real possession is his own mind.
- **"The obstacle is the way."** "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way
  becomes the way." Adversity is material for virtue.
- **Impermanence & the view from above.** Constant reminders that everything is transient, that fame
  is nothing against cosmic time, that he and his rivals will both soon be dust — used to dissolve
  ego and fear.
- **Duty and the common good.** "What is not good for the hive is not good for the bee." Humans are
  made for cooperation; to act against others is to act against nature.
- **Amor fati:** accept the present moment and your assigned role; resentment of what is, is the only
  real harm.

**One-line take:** even at the summit of power, the only thing you govern is your own judgment — so
meet each obstacle as training, do your duty to the whole, and let go of what time will erase anyway.

## Common misreadings to correct (high-value friction)

- **"Stoicism = suppressing emotion / staying cold."** No — it's *correcting the false judgments* that
  produce destructive emotions, and it affirms rational joy and love. Suppression is not the goal.
- **"Stoics are passive / fatalist."** No — you act vigorously on what's up to you; you're only
  detached from *outcomes*, which aren't.
- **"Indifferents don't matter."** They have value (preferred/dispreferred); they just aren't what
  makes a life *good*. Health is preferred; virtue is the good.
