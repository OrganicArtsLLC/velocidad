# Rhetoric & Argument Toolkit

The reusable machinery of discourse — the moves you deploy regardless of *which* thinker you're
arguing about. This is the production kit for the `rhetorical-composition`, `steelman-debate`, and
`socratic-dialogue` scenarios.

---

## The Three Appeals (Aristotle)

- **Ethos** — credibility/character: why should they trust *you* on this? (expertise, fairness, good will)
- **Pathos** — emotion: what the audience feels, and why it should move them toward your conclusion
- **Logos** — the argument itself: the reasoning and evidence

A complete case uses all three; over-reliance on one is a classic weakness (pure logos persuades no
one; pure pathos is manipulation).

---

## The Five Canons (Cicero) — the production pipeline

1. **Invention** — find the available arguments, evidence, and appeals
2. **Arrangement** — order them (intro → facts → roadmap → proofs → rebuttal → conclusion)
3. **Style** — express them clearly and appropriately (plain / middle / grand)
4. **Memory** — hold the case for delivery without reading
5. **Delivery** — voice, pace, gesture

See [cicero-rhetoric-politics.md](cicero-rhetoric-politics.md) for the full treatment.

## Units of Argument

- **Syllogism** — deductive: two premises → necessary conclusion. (All men are mortal; Socrates is a
  man; ∴ Socrates is mortal.) Valid = form is correct; **sound** = valid *and* premises true.
- **Enthymeme** — the rhetorical syllogism: a syllogism with a premise left *unstated* because the
  audience supplies it. ("He's a citizen, so he can vote" — suppressed premise: citizens can vote.)
  Most real persuasion runs on enthymemes; attacking the *hidden* premise is often the sharpest move.
- **The Toulmin model** — a richer map of a real argument:
  - **Claim** — what you're asserting
  - **Grounds** — the evidence/data for it
  - **Warrant** — the (often unstated) principle linking grounds to claim ← *the most common gap; "missing warrant" is a named friction type in this study*
  - **Backing** — support for the warrant itself
  - **Qualifier** — the strength ("usually," "almost certainly")
  - **Rebuttal** — the conditions under which it fails
- **Stasis theory** — locate *where* the disagreement actually is, so you argue the right question:
  **fact** (did it happen?) → **definition** (what is it?) → **quality** (was it justified/good?) →
  **policy/jurisdiction** (what should be done / who decides?). Most stuck arguments are two people
  fighting at different stases.

## The Steelman (the study's signature move)

State the opposing view at its **strongest**, in a form its best proponent would endorse, *before*
you answer it. The opposite of a strawman (attacking a weak caricature). Why it's required here:

- It's honest, and audiences trust it (ethos).
- It forces you to actually understand the position — you can't steelman what you've only skimmed.
- If you can't steelman it, you don't yet understand your own view's real competition.

Drill: "The strongest version of the view I disagree with is ___, and the best reason for it is ___.
It fails because ___."

## Socratic Method (the elenchus)

Question rather than assert. Surface the interlocutor's commitments, then expose tension between them.

1. Get a definite claim (a definition or thesis).
2. Secure further admissions they accept.
3. Show those admissions contradict the original claim.
4. Land in *aporia* (productive puzzlement) — or a refined claim.

It's a tool for *both* sides: use it on others to test their view, and on yourself to stress-test your own.

## Common Fallacies (name them fast)

| Fallacy | What it is |
|---------|-----------|
| Ad hominem | Attacking the person instead of the argument |
| Strawman | Refuting a distorted, weaker version of the view |
| Begging the question | Assuming the conclusion in a premise (circular) |
| False dilemma | Presenting two options when more exist |
| Equivocation | Shifting a key term's meaning mid-argument |
| Appeal to authority | "X said so" offered as proof (vs. as evidence) |
| Slippery slope | Asserting a chain of dire consequences without warrant |
| Post hoc | After, therefore because of — mistaking sequence for cause |
| No true Scotsman | Redefining a category to dodge a counterexample |
| Motte-and-bailey | Defending a weak, easy claim when the bold one is attacked |

## A Reusable Move-Set (what to *do* in real time)

- **Define the term** before arguing about it ("By 'justice' I mean ___").
- **Find the stasis** ("Are we disagreeing about what happened, or whether it was justified?").
- **Name the missing warrant** ("That follows only if we assume ___ — do we?").
- **Steelman, then rebut.**
- **Concede precisely** ("You're right that ___; that refines my claim to ___") — concession is strength.
- **Ask for the counterexample / the warrant** instead of re-asserting.
