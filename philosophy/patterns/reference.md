# Pattern Reference

Reusable argument templates. Master the scaffold, swap the parts, produce hundreds of arguments. A
pattern owned cold is improvisation made cheap: you're not composing structure under pressure, you're
*filling a slot* in a structure you already carry.

This is the **universal engine reference**. Track your personal mastery state in your learner data
directory at `progress/patterns.md`.

**How to use:**
1. Read the scaffold (the formula).
2. Say the first example aloud.
3. Run the swap drill — fill the brackets with a new proposition, thinker, or term.
4. A pattern is mastered when you can fill it with 5+ different contents without hesitation.

---

## Tier 1 — Core Production Patterns

The patterns that carry ~80% of any disciplined exchange. Master these first.

### 1. Claim → Warrant → Support
**Formula:** `[Claim], because [warrant]; you can see it in [support].`
The minimal complete argument — never assert a claim without its warrant.

- "Mandatory voting is justified, because a legitimate mandate requires the whole demos to speak; you
  can see it in how low-turnout results get contested as unrepresentative."
- "The cogito survives the demon, because doubting is itself a form of thinking; you can see it in the
  fact that even 'I might not exist' is a thought being thought."
- "Stoicism isn't suppression, because it targets the *judgment* not the feeling; you can see it in its
  affirmation of rational joy (eupatheiai)."

### 2. Steelman → Pivot → Rebut
**Formula:** `The strongest version of [opposing view] is [X], and its best reason is [Y]. Even granting
that, it fails because [Z].`
The study's signature move. Steelman before you rebut, every time.

- "The strongest case for censoring the speech is that it predictably incites concrete harm, and the
  best reason is that liberty has never been absolute. Even granting that, it fails because the harm
  here is contested and the precedent licenses suppressing any unpopular view."
- "The strongest case for Plato's philosopher-kings is that expertise should govern, as it does in
  medicine. Even granting that, it fails because there's no analogous expertise in *values* that the
  ruled could verify."

### 3. Pin the Term → Define → Proceed
**Formula:** `By [loaded term] I mean [definition]. If we mean different things, that's the disagreement;
if not, then [next step].`
De-noise the field before arguing a loaded word.

- "By 'fair' I mean equal *opportunity*, not equal *outcome*. If you mean outcome, that's our real
  split; if not, then we already agree and can move on."
- "By 'free will' I mean the ability to have done otherwise. Pin that, and the determinism question gets
  sharp instead of endless."

### 4. Concede → Refine
**Formula:** `You're right that [their point]. That doesn't sink my view — it refines it to [narrower,
defensible claim].`
Concession is a weapon: it buys ethos, disarms, and narrows to ground you can hold.

- "You're right that markets fail with externalities. That doesn't sink my view — it refines it to
  'markets are the default *until* a specific externality is shown,' not 'markets always.'"
- "You're right that Marcus was an emperor with power most lack. That refines the claim to 'the
  dichotomy of control scales by station' — not that his situation was ours."

## Tier 2 — Orientation Patterns

Locate the real disagreement before spending words on it.

### 5. Relocate the Stasis
**Formula:** `We're at different stases — are we disagreeing about [fact], [definition], [quality], or
[policy]?`
Most stuck arguments are two people fighting at different levels.

- "Are we disagreeing about whether the layoffs happened, or about whether they were justified? Those
  are different fights."
- "We agree it occurred and what it's called — we're clashing on whether it was *serious*. That's a
  quality dispute, not a factual one."

### 6. Name the Missing Warrant
**Formula:** `That conclusion follows only if we assume [silent premise] — do we grant that?`
The sharpest structural move — it asks the argument to show all its parts without attacking the person.

- "'He's an expert, so he's right' follows only if experts don't err in their field — do we grant
  that?"
- "'It's natural, so it's good' follows only if natural things are reliably good — but plenty aren't."

### 7. Demand the Counterexample
**Formula:** `[Universal claim] — is there a single case where that breaks? If one exists, the claim has
to weaken to [qualified version].`
To kill an "all," you need only one.

- "'Regulation always slows innovation' — is there one case where it accelerated it (safety standards
  enabling trust)? Then it's 'often,' not 'always.'"
- "'No one changes their mind in an argument' — one counterexample and the universal is dead."

## Tier 3 — Canon-as-Ammunition Patterns

Deploy a position as a live move, not a name-drop.

### 8. State a Position (own it cold)
**Formula:** `[Thinker] holds that [claim], because [warrant]. The strongest objection is [counter], to
which the best answer is [response or honest concession].`
This is the ownership test — claim, warrant, objection, all three, in under ten seconds.

- "Aristotle holds that virtue is a mean between extremes, because both excess and deficiency miss the
  good action. The strongest objection is that some acts (murder, betrayal) have no virtuous mean — to
  which Aristotle agrees: the doctrine applies to the passions, not to acts already named as vicious."
- "Schopenhauer holds that reality's inner nature is a blind Will, because we know ourselves from the
  inside as striving. The strongest objection is that 'my inner nature is will' doesn't license 'all of
  reality is Will' — which is a genuine weak point in the inference."

### 9. Apply a Frame
**Formula:** `Run this through [frame]: [how the frame re-sorts the situation], so the move is [action or
reframe].`
Use a position to do work on a live problem.

- "Run this through the dichotomy of control: their decision isn't yours, your response is — so the move
  is to prepare the response, not relitigate the decision."
- "Run this through the golden mean: this isn't 'reckless vs. cowardly,' it's a mean between them — so
  the question is *where* the mean sits here, not which extreme to pick."

### 10. Socratic Definition Probe
**Formula:** `You say [X is Y]. Does that cover [counterexample]? If not, the definition needs to become
[refinement] — or [X] isn't [Y] after all.`
Test a claim by drawing out its own consequences.

- "You say courage is standing firm. Does that cover the soldier who stands firm out of fear of
  punishment? If not, courage needs the *right reason*, not just the act."
- "You say knowledge is true belief. Does that cover a lucky guess that happens to be true? If not, it
  needs justification — true belief *with an account*."

## Tier 4 — Composition Patterns (for rhetorical-composition)

Building a persuasive case, ethically.

### 11. The Arranged Case (Cicero's pipeline, compressed)
**Formula:** `[Hook + why I'm credible] → [the facts] → [my claim] → [proof 1, proof 2] → [strongest
objection, answered] → [close on the claim + why it matters to you].`
Hook(ethos) → narratio → claim → confirmatio → refutatio → peroratio(pathos).

### 12. Wrap the Logos
**Formula:** `[Ethos: I've listened / I concede X] → [Logos: the structured argument] → [Pathos: here's
why this matters for what *you* care about].`
The fix for the all-logos failure mode: airtight reasoning persuades no one delivered flat.

- "You've thought about this more than I have, and you're right about the cost (ethos). Still, the
  structure forces this conclusion (logos). And it matters because it's *your* goal that's at risk if we
  get it wrong (pathos)."

## Pattern Drill Protocol

For each pattern during practice:

1. **Say the scaffold aloud** — feel the shape.
2. **Fill it three times** with different propositions/thinkers (no overthinking).
3. **Stress it** — have the agent attack the filled version; refine on the fly.
4. **Time it** — a Tier 1 pattern should fill in under ~10 seconds to count as owned.

When you can fill a pattern cold under a counter-push, it's mastered. Move to the next.
